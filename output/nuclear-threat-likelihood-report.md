# Nuclear Weapon Use in Conflict — Likelihood Assessment
Date: 2026-04-02

## Executive Summary

- The Doomsday Clock stands at **85 seconds to midnight** (January 2026) — the closest it has ever been, driven by nuclear threats, AI risks, and collapsing arms control.
- **New START expired on February 5, 2026**, ending over 50 years of legally binding US-Russia nuclear arms limits with no replacement treaty under negotiation.
- Expert consensus: the probability of nuclear weapon use is **low but meaningfully elevated** compared to the post-Cold War baseline — most analysts place it above 1990s-2010s levels but below Cold War peaks. Even low-probability risks demand attention given catastrophic consequences.

## 1. Current Geopolitical Hotspots

### Russia-Ukraine
- The war remains the primary nuclear flashpoint. Russia has made repeated veiled nuclear threats since 2022 and revised its nuclear doctrine in late 2024 to lower the threshold for use.
- The war of attrition has arguably reduced the likelihood of a sudden nuclear escalation compared to early-war scenarios, but the risk remains non-zero — particularly if Russia faces severe battlefield losses or territorial threats in Crimea.
- NATO-Russia military encounters are increasing in frequency, especially in the Baltic region. British RAF jets scrambled six times in six days during BALTOPS exercises in June 2025.

### Middle East — Iran
- Iran's nuclear program remains a proliferation concern. The Institute for Science and International Security continues probabilistic tracking of Iran's weapons capability.
- Regional instability (Israel-Gaza conflict, broader tensions) increases the risk of miscalculation among nuclear-armed or near-nuclear states.

### China-Taiwan / Indo-Pacific
- China is rapidly expanding its nuclear arsenal, moving toward rough parity with the US and Russia. This creates a three-way strategic dynamic that is harder to stabilize than Cold War bipolarity.
- The overlap of nuclear and conventional systems in the Pacific theater raises the risk of inadvertent escalation — adversaries may struggle to distinguish conventional from nuclear-capable platforms.

### Korean Peninsula
- North Korea continues expanding its nuclear and missile capabilities with limited diplomatic engagement.

## 3. Historical Context — How Close Have We Come?

- **Cuban Missile Crisis (October 1962)**: Widely considered the closest approach to nuclear war. Soviet submarine B-59 nearly launched a nuclear torpedo; only the dissent of officer Vasily Arkhipov prevented it.
- **Able Archer 83 (November 1983)**: NATO exercise that Soviet leadership interpreted as possible cover for a real first strike. Soviet nuclear forces were placed on high alert. Declassified documents (2021) confirmed the severity.
- **1983 Soviet satellite false alarm**: Stanislav Petrov chose not to report what turned out to be a false alarm from the Soviet early-warning system, averting a potential retaliatory launch.
- **Multiple Cold War incidents**: Chatham House identified at least 12 documented close calls during the Cold War, many involving technical malfunctions or misinterpretation of data.

The current period differs from these historical crises in that the risk is not from a single acute standoff but from a **sustained, multi-theater erosion of guardrails**.

## 4. Factors That Increase or Decrease Risk

### Increasing Risk
- **Arms control collapse**: New START's expiration means no legal limits on US-Russian arsenals and no mutual verification/inspection regime — the first time since the early 1970s.
- **Three-way arms race**: China's arsenal expansion creates a trilateral dynamic that is harder to manage with bilateral treaty frameworks.
- **Doctrinal lowering of thresholds**: Russia's revised nuclear doctrine (2024) broadens conditions for use. Pakistan and North Korea maintain first-use postures.
- **Nuclear-conventional entanglement**: Dual-use delivery systems make it difficult for adversaries to assess whether an incoming strike is conventional or nuclear, raising "use it or lose it" pressures.
- **Weakened alliances and extended deterrence doubts**: JD Vance's 2025 Munich speech raised questions about NATO Article 5 credibility, potentially incentivizing allies to pursue independent deterrents.
- **Proliferation pressure**: The collapse of the arms control framework undermines the NPT and could encourage new states to pursue nuclear weapons.
- **AI and autonomous systems**: Speed of AI-driven decision-making may compress the time available for human judgment in crisis situations.

### Decreasing Risk
- **Mutual assured destruction (MAD)**: The fundamental deterrent logic remains intact — all nuclear states understand that use would invite catastrophic retaliation.
- **No first-use norms**: China maintains a stated no-first-use policy; India has a similar posture. These doctrines reduce (though do not eliminate) risk of initiation.
- **War of attrition dynamics**: In Ukraine, the gradual nature of the conflict reduces the likelihood of sudden escalation compared to a rapid battlefield collapse scenario.
- **Historical precedent**: Nuclear weapons have not been used in conflict since 1945 — an 80-year taboo that carries political and normative weight.
- **Diplomatic channels**: Back-channel communications between nuclear powers continue to exist even amid high tensions, providing off-ramps during crises.
- **Civil society and institutional pressure**: Organizations like the Bulletin, ICAN, and national scientific bodies continue to raise awareness and advocate for restraint.

## 5. Bottom Line Assessment

The risk of nuclear weapon use is **not imminent but is the highest it has been in decades**. The convergence of active conflicts involving nuclear states, the collapse of the arms control architecture, expanding arsenals, and weakened alliance structures creates a compounding threat environment. The danger is less about a deliberate first strike and more about **miscalculation, inadvertent escalation, or crisis decision-making under pressure** — the same dynamics that drove the closest historical calls.
